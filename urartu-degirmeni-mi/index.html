Is that a Urartian mill?

of Edremit, on the Menua (Şamram) canal – a marvel of world water engineering that connects Alniunu, the city of the Urartians home to massive stone quarries, with the Kadembas (Kadembastı) region, famous for its vineyards and built by King Menua for his wife Tariria – this mill, although largely ruined, has managed to survive to this day.

The canal that carries water to the mill

The canal that carries water to the mill from the Menua canal, and the stone retaining wall that supports it, stand perfectly intact. The water channel that carries the water down to the millstone is also sturdy; this water channel deserves special mention because it was constructed by drilling holes in the centers of large blocks of stone and stacking them on top of each other. From above, you can see a void extending approximately 4-5 meters down.

stone path next to the mill

The mill's grinding chamber and storage area have completely collapsed, and the millstone is likely buried under the fallen rocks. Although the walls of the chambers have crumbled, their stone foundations are still visible.

Grinding chamber

Next to the mill, there is a stone-paved road running east-west. The road is very old, as evidenced by the weathered condition of the stones. This road was probably used to bring wheat to the mill and transport flour.

Vertical water channel

The stones used in the construction of the mill are quite large and heavy. Given its similarities to other stone structures built by the Urartians, there are many reasons to believe this mill is an Urartian structure.

retaining wall

I wanted to simply write "Urartian mill" and be done with it, but I couldn't. Although the findings point to the Urartian period, we need proof to say anything definitively, and we don't have that clear evidence.
