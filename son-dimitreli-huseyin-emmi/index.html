Kayseri & Koramaz · 14 June 2019

THE LAST UNCLE HÜSEYİN FROM DIMITRE

As I wandered through the narrow streets of the abandoned village, I could hear the sounds of children running around before the village was deserted. I realized that the building, which was once imposing compared to its counterparts but now even had its roof collapsed, might have been the village café, imagining the sound of teaspoons clinking against cups. And with a meaningless sadness, I wandered through the streets of this abandoned village, Turan.

I was looking inside the houses, dilapidated, scattered; these cold-faced rock houses, once someone's warm home, were now cast aside, perhaps the worthless legacy of grandchildren. Weddings held in their streets, Friday prayers in their mosques, laments sung after death—it was as if they had taken refuge in the last remaining stones, hidden there unnoticed. I always feel sad when I see an abandoned village; it's not just the houses that are abandoned, but also the emotions, memories, and experiences. It seems as if those who are abandoned cling to each other, hiding in the narrow streets of the deserted village, among the remaining stones and rocks.

The village is situated on the west-facing slope of the Koramaz Valley, where three enormous rocks, the size of houses, hang suspended at the point where the valley transitions into a plateau. These rocks have repeatedly rolled down in the past, causing damage to people and homes. The villagers, fed up with this situation, were relocated to their current location on the plateau in 1968 by a decision of the Governorship. The question that will always linger in my mind is: couldn't they have developed a harmonious life with the rocks, as our ancestors did for thousands of years, instead of fleeing from them?

Starting in 1968, groups of people began abandoning the village, and eventually, it was completely deserted except for one person. Yes, you read that right, except for one person. As I walked alone through the abandoned streets of Turan, a house caught my eye; its windows and door were intact, still standing tall. In front of it, I saw a man in his 70s, wearing a cap, moving slowly. I approached him to greet him and introduce myself. He told me, barely able to speak, that he had lived in this house, where he stands now, from the day he was born in 1948 until today, and that even though his entire family and the villagers left in 1968, he never considered leaving. I was curious about his story, and I learned it from him and from the villagers.

His name is Hüseyin Dönmez, but the villagers call him Hüseyin Emmi (Uncle Hüseyin). In his own words, he's the last person living in Dimitre. Although Dimitre (now called Turan) moved to the plateau in 1968, Hüseyin Emmi never left Dimitre. He has lived alone in a stone house for 51 years, since he was 20. The house has no electricity or running water; he heats with a stove and uses candles for light. He has four brothers, and their father raised them on a painter's salary. His brothers help him, providing for his needs like food and heating. They've even invited him to the upper village many times, but Hüseyin Emmi has never responded.

Waking up at sunrise every morning, Uncle Hüseyin eats a few pieces of dry bread from his kitchen, if he has any, grabs his water containers, and sets off towards the new village. Along the way, he stops at the old, ruined village fountain, which has now become known as Uncle Hüseyin's Fountain. He places his container under the trickling water and continues walking towards the new village. Walking slowly but steadily due to his age, Uncle Hüseyin seems to be like a nail nailing the narrow, ruined streets of the village to prevent it from disappearing.

He usually spends his days chatting with the villagers at the village coffee shop. Every Friday, mail arrives in the village; it has become a tradition for the postman to entrust all the mail to Uncle Hüseyin. Uncle Hüseyin puts on his PTT (Turkish Post Office) logo hat and begins to walk slowly through the village. All the mail is entrusted to Uncle Hüseyin, and he delivers them one by one to their recipients. In return for his service, Uncle Hüseyin always receives a tip. Evening comes, and Uncle Hüseyin is tired but happy; the mail has been delivered to its owners. With the tips he collected, he stops by the village grocery store, buys some things, and most importantly, buys his cigarettes, and then sets off again into the narrow streets of the dilapidated village, heading back to the village where he has lived alone since 1968.

He greets and chats with the villagers along the way, and the children swarm around Uncle Hüseyin. Uncle Hüseyin, with the last ounce of his strength, goes down to the fountain, takes his water jug, which is only now full after morning, and goes home. He sits on the threshold of the west-facing door of the last house with a smoking chimney in his village, nestled on the west-facing slope of the Koramaz Valley, and watches the sunset with a cigarette he's lit, who knows what thoughts are going through his mind.
