Sheikh Shaban Village and Tombs (Shemulen Gazi)

The village of Sheikh Shaban, with its 500 households at the time, suffered severe damage in the great flood of 1972 and was relocated 15 km to the southwest.

The present-day Şeyh Şaban village settlement gives the impression of being a more recently constructed village; the original village is located 15 km further up.

The village road is unpaved and easily accessible by vehicles with high ground clearance.

There are about 10 families who stay in the village during the summer months for summer pasture purposes; the village has no electricity or running water.

The village is severely thirsty; the only drinking water is brought from the peaks of Mount Erciyes.

In addition, a large pool dating back to Roman times helps solve the animals' water problem.

There are seven figures whose tombs are said to be located in the village; these are:

Below are images from the tomb of Şemulen Gazi.

Three of the aforementioned tombs were built on three high hills, and unfortunately, only the tomb of Şemulen Gazi remains intact today. The tomb of Şemulen Gazi is built on the highest hill, and access to it, which features characteristics of Seljuk architecture, is via a stabilized road that can only be reached by tractors.

Every year, on the first Sunday after schools close, a festival is held here, sheep are sacrificed, and prayers for rain are offered.

Apart from that, the tombs of Omuzu Gürzlü and Şeyh Şaban are also in good condition.

The tomb of Sheikh Shaban is located within the old village.

There is also an old mosque in the village; a photo of its mihrab (prayer niche) is below. It's a very interesting mihrab, unlike anything I've ever seen.

The village and its surroundings are completely covered with forests; the densest forests of Erciyes are located in this region.

Many wild animals, such as pigs, squirrels, rabbits, wolves, snakes, skunks, owls, eagles, and hawks, live in these mountains and forests.

The village was abandoned a long time ago, so the moment you step inside, you get a shiver down your spine because of the ruined houses.

In the Bal Kaya (Balkaya) region, located northwest of the village, it is said that completely natural honey can be found.

Honey is made by bees inside rocks 50-60 meters above the ground.

So far, only one group of mountaineers has come and taken honey; the villagers have no access to it.
