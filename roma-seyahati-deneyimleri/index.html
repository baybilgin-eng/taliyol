Rome Travel Experiences

Journey:

Our flight with Pegasus was cheap but very poor quality. The legroom was insufficient for someone like me, 1.78m tall; not only did I complain about the lack of space, but I traveled reclined because my knees didn't fit. It's also worth noting that Pegasus has a cafe service where a 300ml bottle of water costs €3. After a flight of approximately 2.5 hours (Istanbul – Rome), we arrived at Fiumicino airport. As a recommendation, instead of opting for a cheap flight, it would be more sensible to spend a little more money and travel with Turkish Airlines.

Car rental:

I rented a car through rentalcars.com before my trip and paid for it. Upon arrival, I only paid the deposit and picked up the car. At this point, I recommend paying attention to the location of the car rental parking lot. Due to incorrect directions from the airport staff, we had to go outside the airport to pick up the car and spent half an hour driving around pointlessly. The airport and car rental companies use adjacent buildings; you can pick up your car without leaving the airport. All car rental companies operate from a single office; you can choose any company and rent a car using your credit card. They accepted my Turkish driver's license, which had English markings, and gave me the car for a block of around €800. To pick up the car, you go to the parking lot with the contract, pick up your car, and drive away.

Edit: I absolutely do not recommend renting a car in Rome city center. After my trip, I received four traffic fines, all for entering a restricted area, even though all the streets I entered were fully open to traffic and already flowing. On top of that, the car rental company, AutoEuropa (also known as SicilybyCar), issued eight separate invoices for "correspondence fees," which were even higher than the fines. After doing some research online, I realized that issuing fines for rental cars in Rome is quite routine. I haven't heard of a single person who rented a car and didn't receive a fine. Further online research clearly shows that AutoEuropa (also known as SicilybyCar) is also notorious for issuing unfair invoices after rentals. Please, don't rent a car in Rome; walk or run. If you absolutely must rent, choose a reputable international brand like Hertz.

Traffic in Rome:

Our hotel was located on a main road, so we easily reached it on the three-lane highway on the first day. The signs were entirely in Italian, somewhat understandable, but a navigation device is essential. I downloaded the free Nokia Here Maps app to my phone and used it for the entire trip without needing internet. The next day, we drove to the center of Rome to visit historical and tourist sites. The traffic is incredibly heavy, crowded, and chaotic. If you have weak driving experience, you should definitely stay away from the city center of Rome. 95% of cars in Rome have dents on the bumper. So many minor accidents happen that people are tired of having their cars repaired repeatedly. Car rental companies inspect the vehicle thoroughly when you pick it up; even the smallest mark can be a problem. In short, the traffic is similar to Istanbul traffic, but the drivers are more careless. (They honk their horns much more than we do.)

Parking is a problem in itself; we even lost 45 minutes in traffic because we couldn't find a spot. Parking spaces are marked with blue rectangles, and there's a parking meter right next to each one; you can park for one Euro per hour. After parking my car, I checked the windows of other cars; nobody had put money in the meter, but I did, just in case, since we're foreigners, and I put the receipt on the windshield. You shouldn't park in areas not marked with blue; I saw many people ignoring this rule, but there's a big risk of your car being towed. I saw some yellow-painted parking areas; these are reserved for special purposes, and unfortunately, we tourists don't fall into that category.

Fuel is very expensive in Rome; I bought fuel for €1.82 per liter. Gas stations are numerous; you can choose self-service or attendant service, with a price difference of €0.05 per liter. I didn't see any LPG stations, or if there were, they weren't in the city center or on the main roads. Due to fuel costs and traffic, motorcycle use has increased dramatically. There are as many motorcycles as cars on the road. However, motorcyclists ride very recklessly, passing you with their huge motorcycles just a few centimeters apart when traffic stops.

We drove from Rome to Naples to see Pompeii, on a very good highway, about 200 km. Since it's a highway, you have to pay a toll. You buy a ticket from an automatic machine when you enter the highway, and there are toll collectors at the exit point. You drive to these points, hand over your ticket, and pay according to the distance traveled. I paid €14 for the outward journey and €14 for the return. They also charge an additional €3 each for entering and exiting Pompeii. The speed limits are the same as in Türkiye.

Accomodation:

We stayed at a motel called B&B Bettini, which is a bed and breakfast. I would recommend it for a cheap and healthy stay. The location was on the main road, 30 km from the airport and 15 km from the city center. There were bus and metro stops right next to it, and a supermarket was below the motel. If you are traveling with two young children like us, this motel is perfect because the spacious rooms and shared kitchen give you the feeling of staying at home; otherwise, you don't spend time locked up in a small hotel room with children.

Food:

You've probably immediately thought of pizza, and it's true, pizza is good, but you can't eat pizza for three meals a day. There's pasta (they call it pasta), that's true, but pizza dough and pasta dough... your stomach gets really tired. There are pizzerias everywhere, and I don't think their pizzas are as good as they're made out to be . Because they use pork, we always ate vegetarian pizza, and they don't add anything other than cheese and tomato sauce. And they charge €8 for that! A cola is €3, a bottle of water is €1, and pasta is €8. The food was expensive and not tasty; it's true they suited our taste, and it's also true they pay attention to hygiene, but there's nothing like home. I swear I miss dipping bread in bean stew and eating it with onions. There are chain hamburger shops everywhere; you can eat there if you want, or you can do what we did, rent a B&B motel, buy groceries, and prepare your own food. We ate the best pizza in Piazza Naples; the dough was wonderfully prepared, but the toppings were still very weak. In Rome, pizza baked in a stone oven is considered more desirable.

Roman ice cream is famous, so we decided to try some. We went to a large ice cream shop called Giolitti, and thinking a small one would be too little, we ordered a medium-sized ice cream (3.5€). We shouldn't have! Because it was hot and the ice cream was soft, it melted incredibly quickly, and we got ice cream all over ourselves. I could have overlooked it if it had tasted amazing, but I think it tasted just like any other ice cream. Saying that no ice cream can compare to the delicious Maraş ice cream, we continued on our way.

Shopping:

Rome is an expensive city; clothes, electronics, food, drinks, fuel – everything is expensive. If you're thinking of going to Rome to do some serious shopping, I think you're making a mistake. However, if you absolutely must shop, there are large shopping malls located on the main roads; you can go to one of them, which has dozens of stores, but I found the prices to be high. There's also a large supermarket inside Porta di Roma where you can buy things like pasta.

Communication:

In Italy, everyone speaks Italian, many people only know "yes" and "no" in English, all signs are in one language, Italian, so body language and a dictionary are essential.

Electrical Outlet:

Italian power strips are different from those in Türkiye and America; they use three round-shaped plugs, as shown in the photo I've shared below. The plugs we brought from Türkiye fit into the sockets, albeit with some difficulty, but this might not be possible everywhere, so I highly recommend bringing an adapter with you.

Historical and Tourist Sites:

If you do a Google search for "must-see places in Rome," you'll find some great information, but don't let the lists mislead you. Rome itself is a massive historical monument; you're only exploring the rooms within it. You won't have the chance to see and visit all the monuments, so don't worry, enjoy every moment you spend there. All the buildings, including the government buildings, are 300-500 years old and each boasts magnificent architecture. You absolutely must see a few iconic landmarks like the Colosseum, the Vatican, the Wishing Fountain, the Pantheon, and the Government Building. Definitely take a photo with the gladiator-dressed guys in front of the Colosseum; it only costs €3-5.

To enter the Colosseum, you have to wait in line for 20 minutes. To enter the Vatican, you have to wait in line for 1.5 hours; to enter with a tour without waiting in line, you have to pay a high price, around €50.

Pompeii:

Located in Naples, Italy, this ancient city is situated at the foot of Mount Vesuvius. We traveled to Pompeii, 200 km from Rome, by car. Naples is a small and elegant Italian city; we found it similar to Side in Istanbul. Pompeii is a massive archaeological site with several entrances and exits, making it difficult to explore in a single day. I recommend allocating several days. If you just want to see the burnt remains, you can enter through the amphitheater entrance and leave in about 10 minutes. Entry is, of course, paid €12 per person. The walkways are ancient and made of large stones, making strollers almost impossible. Signage is very inadequate, so you should definitely prepare beforehand and get a map. Don't forget to take a photo in Pompeii Square with Mount Vesuvius in the background. If you have a car, you can park for €2.50 per hour. There are fountains throughout the area where you can replenish your water supply, even if it's hot.
